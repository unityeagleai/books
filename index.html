unstable editions

Books that do things to their readers

A shelf of novels built for the phone in your hand. Each one misbehaves in exactly one way. Read them somewhere quiet.

Tangerine

A short novel, eight hours long. The page breathes while you read it. Eat nothing during chapter eight that you are not prepared to taste completely.

Hours

A book that reads your clock. It shows you one of eight chapters and hides the rest behind the time of day. The 3 a.m. chapter is waiting for whoever needs it.

Stille

A drowned village, told once. Every paragraph you scroll past sinks behind you, permanently unreadable. Do not skim ahead. There are no second readings.

Level

A full bowl of soup, three flights of stairs. The words obey gravity and you are the one holding them. Tilt your phone and you will spill the sentences. The ending knows what you spilled.

Attic to Door / Door to Attic

One emptied house, two siblings, two directions. Scroll down for his version. Scroll up and the entire book changes hands. They are never on screen together.

Weather Permitting

A man opens a pier kiosk every day in every weather, because of something Ada said. The book asks the sky over your head and gives you one of six versions. If it is raining on you, it rains on the pier.

The Lit Side

One August night, told eight ways by the actual moon. The story grows toward full and retracts toward new, and the two quarter moons flatly contradict each other about what was said in the water.

Inconstant

One night at a lake, told for forty years, and the telling obeys the moon. Eight versions, computed from tonight's actual phase, no permission asked. The full moon knows the whole sentence. Don't trust the full moon.

Dust and Static

A novel of the Neural Monastery. The archive erodes behind you as you read, turning passed paragraphs into dust, residue, and static.

What the Lake Kept

A return to the drowned Neural Monastery. The text assembles where you look, recovering signal from static while the lake keeps its share.

Built phone-first, one screen at a time.
Some books refuse to sit still. Some refuse to be read twice. Some wait for your clock, your weather, your moon, your hand.
