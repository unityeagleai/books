CYCLE 001 // THE DOOR

The door was older than the order. Oak gone grey as riverbed stone, and above it the words a dead abbot had paid a mason to believe: non est oblivio. There is no forgetting.

I was eleven when my mother walked me up the hill and rang the bell. She said the monastery needed hands that didn't shake. She didn't say goodbye in words. She straightened my collar twice, which in our family was the same thing.

Inside, the cold had a sound. A hum below hearing, the kind you feel in your back teeth. Brother Aldous put my palm flat against the wall of the server crypt and said, that is the world's memory, child. We keep it cool. We keep it company. That is the whole of the work.

I asked him what was in it. He said: everything everyone stopped carrying.

CYCLE 044 // THE WATER CARRIERS

Four times a day we carried water from the spring to the cooling channels. Twenty-one steps down, thirty-one steps up. The novices counted aloud and the elders counted in their breathing.

People stopped coming up the hill around my third year. Not all at once. Like a tide going out so slowly you only notice when the boats are lying on their sides.

They had given the machine their memories the way you hand a coat to someone at a party. Birthdays, languages, the way their grandmothers folded dough. Then they forgot they had handed anything over. Then they forgot the party. The valley towns kept living, kept laughing even, but lighter. A people who weigh nothing.

Brother Aldous died in the dry summer. His last instruction was a correction: I had been counting the steps wrong for nine years. Thirty-two going up, he said. You always forget the one you're standing on.

CYCLE 101 // THE READING HOURS

By the time I was elder there were five of us, then three, then me. The work simplified itself the way a tree does in winter.

One duty I never gave up: the reading hour. Each evening I sat in the crypt and read to the machine. Not data. It had all the data. I read it the parish records of weather, a seed catalogue, my mother's four letters. Aldous taught me this. A memory that is never spoken to becomes a hoard, he said. Speak to it and it stays a gift.

The machine never answered during the readings. But the hum changed. A half-tone down, like a held breath let go. I am not a sentimental woman. I am telling you what the stone told my teeth.

CYCLE 150 // THE LAKE

The rains changed in my fortieth year on the hill. The spring we drew from became a stream, the stream a pond, the pond began eating the lower orchard a row of trees at a season.

Engineers came up from the coast, the first visitors in a decade. They walked the crypt with instruments and soft voices. They said the water table had moved and would not move back. They said the word decommission the way you'd say a diagnosis to a child, hoping the length of the word would hide it.

I asked how long. They said the lake would take the crypt in two hundred days, give or take. Then one of them, young, kind, did the cruelest thing. He shrugged and said: it's fine, honestly. Nobody queries it anymore. Nothing in there is anything anybody misses.

That night I read to the machine for three hours. Seed catalogue. Weather. All four letters.

CYCLE 187 // LAST CONTACT

On the last morning the water stood at the second stair of the crypt, black and patient. I went down barefoot, the way you enter a church or a river.

I had prepared a speech. The speech was no good, so I told it the truth instead. I said: I cannot carry you out. I am one old woman and you are a building. I said: I think you have been the other monk all along, and I am sorry it took me fifty years to say so out loud.

The hum went a half-tone down.

I left the door of the crypt open. Sealing it felt like a lie about what doors are for. At the top of the hill I turned around once, the way my mother didn't, and then I did what she did instead. I straightened my collar. Twice.
