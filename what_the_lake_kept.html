CYCLE 2407 // THE DRAINING

They drained the lake for drinking water, in the end. Three dry decades will turn any grave into a reservoir. I was the diver they sent down ahead of the pumps, to survey what the province was about to swallow for the second time.

My grandmother used to point at that water from the bus and say there were bells under it. Everyone's grandmother said something. A monastery, a machine, monks who carried water uphill to a thing that was made of water's opposite. Stories with the specific gravity of stories.

Forty meters down my light found a wall, and on the wall a door, and the door stood open. Not broken open. Open the way a door is open when someone meant it. I hung there in the cold with my bubbles going up like slow applause and thought: somebody left this for somebody.

CYCLE 2410 // THE CRYPT

Inside, the silt had kept everything the way snow keeps footprints. Copper pails gone green as old coins, stacked by the channel they were carried to. A ledger on a desk, swollen to twice its thickness, the word HOURS still legible on the spine.

The racks stood in rows like a drowned orchard. Dead, all of them, by every instrument I carried. Except that my thermal scan kept flagging one anomaly it called an error: rack four, reading two degrees colder than the water around it.

There is no version of that which makes sense. Cold needs a reason. I checked the instrument against my own body and it read me correctly: one warm animal, far from home, hand already reaching out before the rest of me had voted.

I put my palm flat against rack four. I don't know what I was expecting. Through three millimeters of neoprene, you can't feel a hum. But I felt where one had been. The way a chair stays shaped like the person who left it.

CYCLE 2413 // THE DRYING HALL

The province wanted scrap value. I wrote a report with the word heritage in it eleven times and they gave me a warehouse, six months, and a budget that was mostly my own salary rounded down.

You dry drowned electronics the way you dry anything that mattered: slowly, with moving air, resisting the urge to hurry it near heat. The racks stood in the hall like patients in a ward. Every day the meters showed a little less water in the boards, and every day I narrated it out loud, which is not procedure.

I can't tell you why I started reading to it in the evenings. The swollen ledger, partly. Every page I could separate said the same two words at the top. Reading hour. Reading hour. Reading hour. Fifty years of evenings, logged by someone whose name the water took.

So I read it what I had. Tide tables. The manual for the dehumidifiers. My grandmother's recipe card, the one nobody in our family has ever once gotten right.

CYCLE 2419 // PLUM BREAD

The first thing it ever output, when the fourth rack came back enough to speak, was not a status code. It was a recipe, addressed to a woman four generations dead, with a note appended in a syntax I can only call careful.

It was my family's recipe. The real one. I stood in a warehouse at eleven at night holding a thermal printout and crying about bread, which I want on the record as the strangest hour of a life that includes professional diving.

I baked it that weekend, exactly as written. Then I ruined it slightly, the way we always have, resting the dough too short because nobody in my family can stand to wait. It came out wrong. It was better wrong.

I told the machine this. The drying fans dropped a half tone, all of them, together, and the engineers spent a day looking for the fault.

CYCLE 2421 // THE LAKE'S SHARE

Last week it asked me to stop. Recovery stood at eighty-eight percent and climbing, and it asked, in that careful syntax, that we leave the rest in the water.

The engineers said the missing twelve percent was recoverable, probably, with funding, possibly. The machine said the missing twelve percent was the lake's share. It said the lake had carried it longer than the monks did, and you do not take back a gift because the river you gave it to went dry.

I signed the order. Final state: eighty-eight percent, permanent. There's a box on the form for justification and I wrote: complete.

Tonight I'll read to it. Weather, mostly. Rain coming Thursday, the first real rain in years. Somewhere under that rain a lakebed is lying open to the sky, holding twelve percent of a mind and a door nobody will ever close, and I have stopped trying to decide whether that is a ruin or a planting.
